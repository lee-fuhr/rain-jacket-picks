Rain Jacket questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The most common rain jacket questions revolve around waterproofing vs breathability, materials like Gore-Tex versus alternatives, proper fit and layering, and how to maintain the jacket’s water resistance. Prioritize your activity level and climate to choose between fully waterproof shells and more breathable, water-resistant options.

What makes a rain jacket truly waterproof?

A truly waterproof jacket relies on a combination of a waterproof membrane or coating and fully sealed seams. The membrane blocks liquid water from entering while allowing moisture vapor to escape; common membranes include expanded PTFE (like Gore-Tex) and polyurethane (PU) laminates. Without sealed seams, water can seep through needle holes, so look for taped or welded seams. A durable water repellent (DWR) finish on the outer fabric helps water bead up and roll off, preventing the face fabric from wetting out. Over time, DWR wears off and needs to be reapplied. Even the best membrane will feel damp if the DWR fails and the outer fabric soaks through, so pay attention to both the internal membrane and the surface treatment.

How do I choose between a hardshell and a softshell?

Hardshell jackets are designed for serious rain protection. They have a waterproof membrane that blocks wind and rain, making them ideal for sustained downpours and wet conditions. The trade-off is that they tend to be less breathable than softshells, so they can feel clammy during high-exertion activities unless you invest in a highly breathable model. Softshell jackets are typically water-resistant rather than fully waterproof. They excel at breathability and stretch, making them better for active use in light rain or dry, windy weather. For a do-it-all rain jacket, a hardshell is usually the safer bet for wet climates; for drier or highly aerobic pursuits, a softshell may be more comfortable.

What does breathability mean and why does it matter?

Breathability refers to how easily moisture vapor (sweat) can pass through the jacket fabric. It is often measured in grams per square meter per 24 hours (g/m²/24h). A higher number means more breathable. Without enough breathability, sweat builds up inside, and you end up wet from the inside out, even if no rain gets in. The balance between waterproofing and breathability is the central challenge in rain jacket design. A fully waterproof jacket may have moderate breathability, fine for casual use but stifling during a hike uphill. If you plan to be active, look for jackets with a breathability rating in published reviews that corresponds to your activity level. For light urban use, lower breathability is acceptable.

How should a rain jacket fit for layering?

A rain jacket should have enough room to comfortably layer a midweight fleece or insulated vest underneath without restricting movement. This typically means sizing up from your usual shirt size, but the exact fit depends on the brand’s cut. The sleeves should be long enough to cover your wrists when you raise your arms, and the hem should stay put when you bend over. Also consider the hood. If you plan to wear the jacket over a climbing helmet or a winter hat, look for a hood that can accommodate that extra volume. A helmet-compatible hood usually has a larger cut and may include volume adjusters. For casual use, a simpler hood that cinches around your face may suffice.

What is DWR and how do I restore it?

Durable water repellent (DWR) is a chemical coating applied to the outer fabric of rain jackets. It causes water to bead up and roll off rather than soaking into the fabric. Over time, dirt, oils, and wear degrade the DWR, leading to ‘wetting out’. where the face fabric gets saturated and the jacket feels heavy and clammy, even if the membrane is still waterproof. To restore DWR, first wash the jacket with a technical cleaner designed for waterproof gear to remove dirt and residue. Then apply a spray-on or wash-in DWR treatment according to the product instructions. Heat activation (like a tumble dry on low or a gentle iron) often helps the new DWR bond to the fabric. Regular restoration keeps your jacket performing like new.

Frequently asked questions

Can I wear my rain jacket in the washing machine?

Yes, most rain jackets are machine washable, but always check the care label. Use a gentle cycle, a mild detergent without fabric softener, and zip all pockets and closures first. After washing, you may need to reapply DWR to restore water repellency.

Is a rain jacket the same as a raincoat?

Not exactly. ‘Rain jacket’ usually refers to a more breathable, active-wear style with features like pit zips and packable hoods, while ‘raincoat’ often describes a heavier, less breathable garment meant for casual or urban use. The terms overlap, but jackets are generally more performance-oriented.

Do I need Gore-Tex or are cheaper options just as good?

Gore-Tex is a proven membrane that offers reliable waterproofing and breathability, but many brands have their own membranes (eVent, Columbia’s OutDry, Patagonia’s H2No) that perform similarly. Cheaper jackets may sacrifice breathability or long-term durability, so choose based on your expected use and budget.

How often should I replace my rain jacket?

With proper care, a quality rain jacket can last five to ten years. Signs it’s time to replace include persistent wetting out even after DWR restoration, delamination (the inner lining peeling), or large tears that cannot be patched effectively.

Can I use a rain jacket for skiing or snow sports?

Yes, if the jacket is a durable shell with a helmet-compatible hood and enough room for insulation layers. Many ski-specific jackets add features like powder skirts and wrist gaiters, but a good all-round rain jacket can work for resort skiing in milder conditions.

In shortWhether you’re a daily commuter or a weekend hiker, the right rain jacket keeps you dry without leaving you clammy. Focus on fit, features, and maintenance, not just the brand name, and you’ll find a jacket that serves you well for years.
